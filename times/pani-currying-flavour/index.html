    Pani: Currying Flavour

    A rendering of the interior of Pani, the new Indian restaurant opening on the Camana Bay Crescent in June.

    Camana Bay News

    June 2017
    By Alan Markoff

    Camana Bay News

    June 2017 - Alan Markoff

    The authentic cuisine of India, a country where regional flavours and cooking styles are as diverse as Italy, Spain or France, involves much more than just curry.

    Pani, a new Market Street Group restaurant opening in Camana Bay in June, seeks to present an authentic representation of Indian cuisine and although curry will certainly be a part of its offerings, there will be many other dishes on the menu.

    “One of our best dishes is rogan josh,” says Pani’s head chef Prabu Natarajan. “It’s a dish that comes from Kashmir and we’re going to make it with lamb shank.”

    Pani will be the Market Street Group’s fourth restaurant in Camana Bay, along with The Waterfront Urban Diner, Mizu Asian Bistro + Bar and The Brooklyn Pizza + Pasta. Manager Jeff Stewart says the idea for the restaurant came from the fact that more than 40 Indian chefs work for the various Market Street Group restaurants, which also include other Grand Cayman eateries Craft Food & Beverage Co., Dukes Seafood & Rib Shack, Lone Star Bar & Grill and Boggy Sand Cafe.

    Manager Jeff Stewart says the idea for the restaurant came from the fact that more than 40 Indian chefs work for the various Market Street Group restaurants.

    “We were having a sit-down discussing what we were going to do next and it just came to us to do an Indian restaurant,” Stewart says. “We have so much [Indian] talent in the kitchens, it just made sense.”

    During the planning, ordering and fit-out stage of the process of opening the new restaurant, the Market Street Group was able to have a trial run by offering a weekly “Curry Night” at The Waterfront for the past nine months, Stewart says.

    “It gave us an opportunity to see what works and what doesn’t work,” he says. “We were lucky to have a test restaurant to do that in.” The result will be an Indian restaurant that balances the types of curries loved in the UK and in Cayman – including Britain’s unofficial national dish, chicken tikka masala – with some authentic dishes from various parts of India.

    “It will be mostly northern Indian dishes, which are richer and cream-based,” says Natarajan. “But there will be dosas and some other southern Indian dishes, too. And we’ll also have some real Indian street food on the menu.” The menu will be so authentic that guests may not even recognise the names of some of the dishes, like murgh mathi malai, dal makhni or kozhi rasam. However, there will be mainstream Indian restaurant dishes like pork vindaloo, madras beef curry and mulligatawny soup.

    Some of the dishes will only be served spicy, while some can be ordered to preference, Stewart says, noting that to make some of the dishes authentic, the chefs need to stay true to the heat called for in the dish.

    “The chefs are going to come at this with the [heat] level they think is appropriate,” he says. One big focus on the menu will be tandoori and Pani has incorporated two large tandoor ovens – one for breads and one for meats – in its kitchen.

    “Guests will be able to see the chefs at work at those ovens,” Stewart says. “The tandoors will be the heart of our kitchen.” In addition to the meats and breads, Pani will have many vegetarian side dishes and main course dishes as well. The chefs have also experimented with the creation of some nontraditional fusion dishes.

    “We have a beef short rib marsala that is out of this world,” Stewart says.

    INDIA’S INFLUENCE

    There’s good reason beyond Cayman’s love of curries why there are so many Indian chefs
    in Cayman. “They are professionals in every sense of the word,” says Stewart. “They know what is expected of them and you can count on them.”

    That professionalism comes from training. Chefs in India all go to culinary school. “The first year, we start with French cooking and learn all the techniques,” says Natarajan. “The second year, we learn about all the different kinds of Indian food. The third year, we learn the cuisine of everywhere else.”

    Indian culinary schools are very regimented and produce highly capable chefs who can market their talents in restaurants around the globe. Many of the Indians living in Cayman work in the kitchens of restaurants. Indian chefs often win the Cayman Culinary Society’s annual “Chef of the Year” competition.

    One of the early Indian chefs to make a name for himself in Cayman was Vidyadhara Shetty, who started as a breakfast chef at the old Hyatt Regency, eventually became the executive chef for that hotel and now owns two restaurants on Grand Cayman, including Blue Cilantro, which was named the Best Caribbean Restaurant in 2015 at Jamaica Observer Table Talk Food Awards in Kingston.

    Although Blue Cilantro offers a fusion of Indian, Caribbean and Mediterranean cuisine, Chef Shetty’s other restaurant – the Health City Cafe by Blue Cilantro – may be one of the best restaurants in a hospital on the planet and attracts residents and those working in the Eastern Districts of Grand Cayman for lunch and dinner.

    Other places for Indian cuisine on Grand Cayman include Southern Spice Restaurant, Seaharvest Restaurant, George Town Yacht Club on Tuesday nights, Icoa on Thursday nights and Gateway of India, which, although the sit-down restaurant closed, still offers food for pick-up and delivery.

    Once it opens its doors this month, Pani’s will serve lunch and dinner between 11:30am and 10pm, daily.
